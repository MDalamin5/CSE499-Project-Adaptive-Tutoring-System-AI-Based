
***Simplify as much as possible \(3x(x - 1) - 7x^2\)


#### **Variant 1 (Direct Understanding)**

**Student**: Simplify \(3x(x - 1) - 7x^2\).  
**System**: Start by expanding \(3x(x - 1)\):  
\[
3x(x - 1) = 3x^2 - 3x
\]  
Substituting this gives:  
\[
3x^2 - 3x - 7x^2
\]  
Combine like terms (\(3x^2 - 7x^2\)):  
\[
-4x^2 - 3x
\]  
Do you understand this step?  
**Student**: Yes, I got it.  
**System**: Great! The final simplified expression is:  
\[
-4x^2 - 3x
\]



#### **Variant 2 (Hints for Combining Like Terms)**

**Student**: Simplify \(3x(x - 1) - 7x^2\).  
**System**: First, expand \(3x(x - 1)\). Do you know how to distribute \(3x\)?  
**Student**: Not sure, can you give me a hint?  
**System**: Sure! Multiply \(3x\) by both terms inside the parentheses: \(3x \cdot x = 3x^2\) and \(3x \cdot -1 = -3x\). So, \(3x(x - 1) = 3x^2 - 3x\).  
**Student**: Oh, I see. So it’s \(3x^2 - 3x - 7x^2\).  
**System**: Exactly! Now combine the \(x^2\)-terms: \(3x^2 - 7x^2 = -4x^2\). The final expression is \(-4x^2 - 3x\).



#### **Variant 3 (Request for Alternative Method)**

**Student**: Simplify \(3x(x - 1) - 7x^2\).  
**System**: One way is to expand \(3x(x - 1)\) into \(3x^2 - 3x\), then subtract \(7x^2\) to get \(-4x^2 - 3x\).  
**Student**: Is there another way to look at this?  
**System**: Sure! You can factor out \(x\) first:  
\[
3x(x - 1) - 7x^2 = x(3(x - 1) - 7x).
\]  
Simplify inside the parentheses:  
\[
3(x - 1) = 3x - 3, \quad \text{so } 3x - 3 - 7x = -4x - 3.
\]  
Thus, the factored form is \(x(-4x - 3)\), which expands to \(-4x^2 - 3x\).  



#### **Variant 4 (Misunderstanding Combining Terms)**

**Student**: Oh, I see now. Thanks!  
**System**: Great! The simplified expression is \(-4x^2 - 3x\).

# **Variant 3 (Request for Alternative Method)**
**Student**: Simplify \((2x)^2 - 2x^2\).
**System**: Sure! One way is to expand \((2x)^2\) as \(4x^2\). Then we subtract \(2x^2\), leaving \(2x^2\).  
**Student**: That seems too direct. Is there another way to look at it?  
**System**: Absolutely! We could also think of factoring out \(2x^2\) from the start. Observe:  
\[
(2x)^2 - 2x^2 = 2x^2 \left( \frac{(2x)^2}{2x^2} - 1 \right).
\]
But first, we have to rewrite \((2x)^2\) carefully.  
**Student**: So \((2x)^2 = 4x^2\). Then factoring out \(2x^2\) from \(4x^2\) would be \(2 \cdot x^2 \cdot 2 = 2x^2 \times 2\). Let me think.  
**System**: That’s correct. If you factor \(2x^2\) out of \(4x^2\), you get \(2\). If you factor \(2x^2\) out of \(-2x^2\), you get \(-1\). So inside the parentheses, you’d have \(2 - 1 = 1\). Multiply that by \(2x^2\), and you still get \(2x^2\).  


# **Variant 4 (Misunderstanding Concept of Combining Like Terms)**

**Student**: Simplify \((2x)^2 - 2x^2\).  
**System**: First, expand \((2x)^2\) to get \(4x^2\). Then subtract \(2x^2\), leaving \(2x^2\).  
**Student**: Wait, how are we allowed to just combine \(4x^2\) and \(-2x^2\)? Are they really the same kind of term?  
**System**: Good question! Yes, they’re both \(x^2\)-terms. Combining like terms means we add/subtract their coefficients. Here, \(4 - 2 = 2\).  
**Student**: Oh, that makes sense. So it’s \(2x^2\).  
**System**: Exactly! The simplified result is \(2x^2\).
